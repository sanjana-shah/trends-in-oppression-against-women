More than 1 in 3 women (35.6%) in the U.S. will experience rape, physical violence and/or stalking by an intimate partner in their lifetime.

On a typical day, there are more than 20,000 phone calls placed to domestic violence hotlines nationwide.

Most cases of domestic violence are never reported to the police.

Problem Statement

There is an ongoing discussion about woman's right in Iran and all over the world in solidarity. While these stats are disheartening and hint at a larger problem, we want to explore the theory that one of the reasons that fewer women speak up is because women believe that they deserve it, that they deserved to be beaten up she burns the food; she goes out without telling him

We are in no way attempting to shift the blame to the victim. We understand the various reason why a person would remain in an abusive situation and are sympathic to the same. This is just a data exploration essay where several men and women from different countries were asked questions like "A husband is justified in hitting or beating his wife if she refuses to have sex with him" or "A husband is justified in hitting or beating his wife if she neglects the children" and are only attempting to visualize that data. We also acknowledge that the problem isn't only limited to women, and people of all genders can experience sexual violence, however for the scope this essay, we would limit our discussion to cis-women.

But really, what are the variables affecting these numbers?

Are developed countries better than developing countries?

Presenting comparison between two countries: A developed one (USA) and a developing one (India)
Here, one can see that both the countries have the same score on how far does the legal framework offers women legal protection from domestic violence. Also, both the countries score about the same on prevalence of violence in the lifetime and attitudes toward violence. So while, being more developed does make women think that they are less deserving or abuse, it doesn't necessarily reduce the abuse.

There's a direct correlation between how worse the violence is with how justifiable the women think that violence is.

Why is education such a big deal?

Out of all the various demographics, education appears to be the largest factor on the perception of violence. As the education level of the person raises, the agreement of justifiable violence goes down.

How are other countries doing in this?

Using this interactive graph, we are able to set certain questions, demographic, and country to see how the respondent are compared to the other sub-demographics when it comes to their perception of justifiable violence. We found that education level seems to be the biggest and most consistent factor. The higher the education, the higher chance that they will not agree to the violence being justified no matter the question.
